Chapter 01
Introduction to Management Accounting
01
Financial vs Cost vs Management Accounting
▶
Financial Accounting
Records past transactions and prepares financial statements for external users (investors, banks, govt).
Cost Accounting
Tracks and records all costs of producing a product or service — used internally to control costs.
Management Accounting
Provides financial AND non-financial info to internal managers for Planning, Control, and Decision-Making.
Cost = money paid to produce something. All expenses are costs, but not all costs are expenses.
Financial accounting = external focus. Management accounting = internal focus.
02
Three Purposes of Management Accounting
▶
Planning — Setting goals and preparing budgets for future activities.
Control — Comparing actual results vs budget, then taking corrective action.
Decision Making — Choosing the best option from available alternatives using relevant info.
03
3 Planning Levels
▶
Strategic — Long-term, top management, unstructured decisions (e.g. enter new market).
Managerial (Tactical) — Medium-term, middle management, bridge between strategic & operational.
Operational — Short-term, day-to-day, detailed decisions (e.g. rejects per machine).
04
Qualities of Good Information (ACCURATE)
▶
Accurate — Free from errors, degree of accuracy depends on purpose.
Complete — All needed info is there, but not excessive.
Cost-beneficial — Cost of info should not exceed its value.
Understandable — Easy to understand by the user.
Relevant — Only useful info, no irrelevant details.
Timely — Available when needed, not too late.
Accessible / Accountable — Easy to retrieve, with clear responsibility.
05
ICMA Pakistan
▶
Established in 1951, granted statutory status under Cost & Management Accountants Act 1966.
Sole provider of cost and management accounting education in Pakistan.
📝 Chapter Summary

Management accounting supplies financial and non-financial information to internal managers for planning, controlling, and decision-making — unlike financial accounting (external, regulated, historical) and cost accounting (cost determination only).

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Financial Accounting = external users, statutory format, past-focused. Management Accounting = internal, no fixed format, future-focused.
  • Cost accounting data feeds directly into management accounting reports.
  • Core functions: Planning, Controlling, Decision-Making, Performance Evaluation.
  • Management accountants sit closest to policy/decision makers — not external auditors.
Chapter 02
Sources of Data
01
Data vs Information
▶
Data
Raw facts and figures that have not been processed yet. (e.g. list of numbers)
Information
Processed data that has meaning and is useful for decision-making.
02
Primary vs Secondary Data
▶
Primary Data — Collected first-hand for a specific purpose (e.g. surveys, interviews, experiments).
Secondary Data — Already collected by someone else (e.g. government reports, published accounts).
Primary = more accurate but expensive. Secondary = cheaper but may be outdated.
03
Sampling Methods
▶
Random Sampling — Every item has equal chance of selection. Most unbiased method.
Systematic Sampling — Select every nth item from a list (e.g. every 10th customer).
Stratified Sampling — Divide population into groups (strata), then randomly sample each group.
Cluster Sampling — Divide into clusters, randomly select entire clusters. Cheaper but less accurate.
Multi-stage Sampling — Sampling done in stages, each stage using random selection.
Quota Sampling — Non-random; interviewer fills a set quota from each group. Quick but biased.
04
Internal vs External Information
▶
Internal — Comes from within the organisation (e.g. payroll, sales records, production reports).
External — Comes from outside (e.g. market reports, competitor info, government statistics).
📝 Chapter Summary

Before data can support decisions it must be sourced, sampled appropriately, and processed into meaningful information.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Primary data = collected first-hand for the specific purpose. Secondary data = reused from existing sources.
  • 5 sampling methods: random, systematic, stratified, cluster, multi-stage — stratified is the most representative.
  • Quantitative data = numeric (measurable); Qualitative data = descriptive (opinions, characteristics).
  • Data (raw, no meaning) → Processing → Information (organised, meaningful).
Chapter 03
Cost Classification & Cost Behaviour
01
Direct vs Indirect Costs
▶
Direct Cost
Can be clearly traced to a specific product/job. (e.g. raw material used in product, wages of factory worker)
Indirect Cost (Overhead)
Cannot be traced directly — shared across many products. (e.g. factory rent, supervisor salary)
Prime Cost = Direct Material + Direct Labour + Direct Expenses.
02
Classification by Function
▶
Production Cost — Costs incurred in the factory to manufacture goods.
Selling Cost — Costs of promoting and selling products (e.g. advertising, salesmen commission).
Distribution Cost — Costs of delivering goods to customers (e.g. delivery vehicle, road tax).
Administration Cost — General management costs (e.g. chairman's PA salary, office rent).
Finance Cost — Cost of borrowing (e.g. loan interest, bank charges).
03
Cost Behaviour (Fixed, Variable, Semi-Variable)
▶
Fixed Cost
Stays the same regardless of output level. (e.g. rent Rs 50,000 — doesn't change if you make 100 or 1000 units)
Variable Cost
Changes in direct proportion to output. (e.g. raw materials — more units = more material cost)
Semi-Variable (Mixed) Cost
Has a fixed part + variable part. (e.g. phone bill = fixed line rental + variable call charges)
Stepped Fixed Cost — Fixed until a certain level, then jumps up (e.g. need extra supervisor after 100 workers).
04
High-Low Method (Separating Fixed & Variable)
▶
Used to split semi-variable costs into fixed and variable parts.
Variable Cost per Unit
(High Cost − Low Cost) ÷ (High Units − Low Units)
Fixed Cost
Total Cost at any level − (Variable rate × Units)
05
Product Cost vs Period Cost
▶
Product Cost — Attached to the product; included in inventory until sold (e.g. material, labour, production overhead).
Period Cost — Charged to P&L in the period incurred regardless of sales (e.g. admin, selling expenses).
📝 Chapter Summary

Costs are classified by element, function, nature, and behaviour — this classification underpins every later costing technique in the syllabus.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Prime Cost = Direct Material + Direct Labour + Direct Expenses. Conversion Cost = Direct Labour + Overheads.
  • Fixed costs are constant IN TOTAL; variable costs are constant PER UNIT.
  • Semi-variable (mixed) costs = fixed element + variable element — split using the High-Low method.
  • Product costs are inventoried (held in stock value); period costs are expensed in the period incurred.
Chapter 04
Materials: Accounting & Control
01
Inventory Valuation Methods
▶
FIFO (First In, First Out)
Oldest stock is issued first. Closing stock = latest (most recent) prices. In rising prices → higher closing stock, higher profit.
LIFO (Last In, First Out)
Newest stock issued first. Closing stock = oldest prices. Not allowed under IAS 2.
AVCO (Weighted Average Cost)
Average cost calculated after each receipt. Issues at average price. Smooths out price fluctuations.
AVCO Formula
New Average = (Old Stock Value + New Purchase Value) ÷ Total Units
02
Stores Documentation
▶
Purchase Requisition — Request raised by stores/department to buy materials.
Purchase Order — Official order sent by buying dept to supplier.
Goods Received Note (GRN) — Records materials received from supplier; used to check against invoice.
Material Requisition Note — Used by production dept to request materials from stores.
Material Transfer Note — When material is moved between departments.
Material Return Note — Unused material returned back to stores.
03
Inventory Count (Stocktaking)
▶
Periodic Stocktaking — Count all stock at one time (usually year-end). Expensive and time-consuming.
Continuous/Perpetual Inventory — Count different items throughout the year. Each item counted at least once a year.
Inventory Discrepancy — When physical count differs from bin card / stores ledger. Needs investigation.
📝 Chapter Summary

Material accounting tracks the full cycle from purchase to issue, with proper documentation and valuation methods to support accurate costing.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Document flow: Purchase Requisition → Purchase Order → GRN → Material Requisition Note → Bin Card update.
  • Valuation methods: FIFO, LIFO, Weighted Average (AVCO) — each gives a different closing stock value.
  • Material control aims for the right quantity, time, price and quality.
  • Normal material loss is absorbed into good units' cost; abnormal loss is charged to P&L.
Chapter 05
Materials: Inventory Control
01
Costs of Holding Inventory
▶
Holding Costs — Storage, insurance, obsolescence, tied-up capital. Increase as order quantity increases.
Ordering Costs — Admin cost of placing each order. Decrease as order quantity increases (fewer orders needed).
Stockout Cost — Lost sales / production stoppage when stock runs out.
Purchase Price — Cost of buying the items (may get bulk discount).
02
EOQ — Economic Order Quantity KEY
▶
EOQ
The order quantity that minimises total inventory costs (holding + ordering). At EOQ, holding cost = ordering cost.
EOQ Formula
EOQ = √(2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost ÷ Holding Cost per unit per year)
Higher holding cost → smaller EOQ (order less, more frequently).
Higher demand or ordering cost → larger EOQ.
03
Stock Level Formulas KEY
▶
Reorder Level (ROL)
Maximum Usage × Maximum Lead Time
Minimum Level (Buffer Stock)
Reorder Level − (Average Usage × Average Lead Time)
Maximum Level
Reorder Level + EOQ − (Minimum Usage × Minimum Lead Time)
Average Stock
Minimum Level + ½ EOQ
Lead Time = Time between placing an order and receiving delivery.
04
ABC Analysis of Inventory
▶
A items — High value, few items (~20% of items = ~80% of value). Tight control needed.
B items — Medium value. Moderate control.
C items — Low value, many items. Loose control, bulk ordering fine.
05
Just-In-Time (JIT) Inventory
▶
JIT — Materials arrive exactly when needed; no buffer stock held. Eliminates holding costs.
Requires reliable suppliers and short lead times. Very risky if supplier fails.
Reduces waste and storage costs but increases risk of stockout.
📝 Chapter Summary

Inventory control techniques like EOQ, reorder levels, and safety stock minimise total inventory cost (ordering + holding) while preventing stockouts.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • EOQ = √(2 × D × Co ÷ Ch) — the order quantity that minimises total ordering + holding cost.
  • Reorder Level = Maximum usage × Maximum lead time (covers worst-case before next delivery arrives).
  • Safety stock is the buffer held against unexpected demand or delivery delays.
  • Maximum level = ROL + ROQ − (Min usage × Min lead time); Minimum level = ROL − (Avg usage × Avg lead time).
Chapter 06
Accounting for Labour
01
Remuneration Methods
▶
Time Rate
Paid for hours worked regardless of output. Stable income but no incentive to work faster. (e.g. Rs 200/hour)
Piece Rate
Paid per unit produced. More output = more pay. Incentivises speed but may reduce quality. (e.g. Rs 50 per unit)
Bonus / Premium Schemes
Basic time rate + bonus if output exceeds standard. Combines stability with incentive.
02
Overtime & Idle Time
▶
Overtime = Hours worked beyond normal hours, paid at higher rate (e.g. time + half, double time).
Overtime Premium = Extra amount paid above normal rate. Normally treated as indirect cost (overhead).
Exception: if overtime is done at customer's specific request, premium is charged as direct cost.
Idle Time = Workers paid but not producing (e.g. machine breakdown, waiting for materials).
Idle time is always an indirect cost (overhead). Shown as adverse variance.
Labour Efficiency %
(Expected Hours ÷ Actual Hours) × 100
03
Labour Turnover
▶
Labour Turnover
Rate at which employees leave and are replaced. High turnover = high recruitment + training costs.
Labour Turnover Rate
(No. of leavers replaced ÷ Average no. of employees) × 100
Causes include: low pay, poor conditions, better opportunities elsewhere.
04
Labour Cost Documents
▶
Time Sheet — Records hours spent on each job/activity by each worker daily/weekly.
Job Card — Attached to a specific job, records time and materials used for that job.
Clock Cards / Attendance Records — Record arrival/departure time to calculate total hours worked.
05
Bonus Incentive Schemes — Halsey, Rowan, 100% Bonus KEY
▶
Halsey Premium Plan (50% bonus)
Earnings = (Actual Hours × Rate) + 50% × (Time Saved × Rate)
Time Saved = Standard Hours Allowed − Actual Hours Worked
Rowan Plan
Earnings = (Actual Hours × Rate) + (Time Saved ÷ Standard Time) × Actual Hours × Rate
100% Bonus Plan
Standard Hours Allowed = Actual Output ÷ Standard Output per Hour
Bonus = Time Saved × Hourly Rate
Total Earnings = Basic Wages + Bonus
Labour Cost per Unit = Standard Labour Cost per Unit (always!)
Additional Production Bonus (Arshad/Badar type)
Threshold = e.g. 80% of Standard Output
Bonus = Rs. X for each % point actual exceeds threshold
Extra units above threshold ÷ 1% of standard = bonus units
e.g. Standard=1000, Threshold=800, Actual=890 → (890-800)=90 units → Rs.900 bonus
100% Bonus Plan key rule: Labour cost per unit is always equal to the standard cost regardless of actual output. The bonus exactly compensates for time saved.
Rowan vs Halsey: When time saved > 50% of standard time, Rowan gives LESS bonus than Halsey — protects employer from loosely set standards.
All bonus plans share a common goal: reward workers who beat standard time, while guaranteeing at least the basic time-rate wage.
📝 Chapter Summary

Labour cost accounting covers payment methods, incentive schemes, and the correct classification of direct vs indirect labour, idle time and overtime.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Direct labour → debited to WIP. Indirect labour, overtime premium and idle time → Factory Overhead Control.
  • Halsey Plan: bonus = 50% of time saved × rate. Rowan Plan: bonus = (time saved ÷ standard time) × actual hours × rate.
  • Labour Turnover = Leavers ÷ Average Workforce × 100.
  • 100% Bonus Plan: labour cost per unit ALWAYS equals the standard cost, regardless of efficiency.
Chapter 07
Accounting for Overheads
01
What are Overheads?
▶
Overhead
Indirect costs that cannot be traced to a specific product. Includes: indirect material, indirect labour, indirect expenses.
Overheads must be absorbed into product costs using a fair basis (called absorption rate).
02
Overhead Absorption Rate (OAR) KEY
▶
OAR Formula
OAR = Budgeted Overhead ÷ Budgeted Activity Level
Calculated at the start of the period using budgeted figures (not actual).
Common bases: Direct Labour Hours, Machine Hours, Units Produced, % of Prime Cost.
Labour-intensive production → use Labour Hours basis. Machine-intensive → use Machine Hours.
03
Over / Under Absorption
▶
Over-Absorption
Overhead absorbed > Actual overhead. Excess is added back to profit (favourable).
Under-Absorption
Overhead absorbed < Actual overhead. Shortfall is deducted from profit (adverse).
Absorbed Overhead
OAR × Actual Activity Level
04
Overhead Allocation, Apportionment & Reapportionment
▶
Allocation — Overhead directly charged to a specific cost centre (e.g. depreciation of machine in Dept A).
Apportionment — Shared overhead split across departments on a fair basis (e.g. rent by floor area, power by machine hours).
Reapportionment — Service dept costs transferred to production depts (service depts don't make products).
OverheadApportionment Basis
Rent & RatesFloor Area
PowerMachine Hours / kWh
Insurance of AssetsAsset Values
Canteen CostsNumber of Employees
DepreciationAsset Values
📝 Chapter Summary

Overheads are allocated or apportioned to cost centres, then absorbed into products using a predetermined OAR; any over/under-absorption is adjusted at year-end.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Allocation = charging a whole cost to ONE cost centre. Apportionment = sharing a common cost across MULTIPLE centres on a fair basis.
  • Service department costs are re-apportioned to production departments (direct, step-down, or reciprocal method).
  • OAR = Budgeted Overhead ÷ Budgeted Activity Level — calculated BEFORE the period begins.
  • Over/Under absorption = Applied (Absorbed) OH − Actual OH; the difference is written off to the costing P&L.
Chapter 08
Absorption & Marginal Costing
01
Marginal Costing KEY
▶
Marginal Cost
The variable cost of producing one more unit. Fixed costs are not included in product cost.
Contribution
Sales Revenue − Variable Costs. Contribution goes towards covering fixed costs and then profit.
Marginal Costing Profit Statement
Sales − Variable Costs = Contribution → Contribution − Fixed Costs = Profit
02
Absorption Costing
▶
Absorption Costing
Both fixed AND variable production costs are included in product cost. Fixed overheads are absorbed into each unit.
Required by IAS 2 for financial reporting purposes.
Absorption Costing — Product Cost
Direct Material + Direct Labour + Direct Expense + Fixed + Variable Production OH
03
Key Differences: Marginal vs Absorption
▶
PointMarginalAbsorption
Fixed CostsPeriod cost (all to P&L)Product cost (some in stock)
Closing Stock ValueLowerHigher
Profit when production > salesLowerHigher
Profit when production = salesSameSame
Required by IAS 2?NoYes
Better for decisions?YesNo
Profit Reconciliation
Marginal Profit ± (Change in Stock × Fixed OH per unit) = Absorption Profit
📝 Chapter Summary

Absorption costing includes fixed overhead in product cost (affects inventory valuation); marginal costing treats fixed overhead as a period cost and focuses on contribution for decisions.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Absorption Profit = Marginal Profit + (Change in inventory units × Fixed OH rate per unit).
  • Contribution = Sales − Variable Costs (the basis for all marginal costing decisions).
  • Marginal costing is preferred for CVP analysis, make-or-buy, and special-order decisions.
  • If Production = Sales (no inventory change), both methods give the SAME net profit.
Chapter 09
Job & Batch Costing
01
Job Costing
▶
Job Costing
Each customer order/job is unique and costs are collected separately for each job. (e.g. custom furniture, construction)
Each job gets its own Job Cost Card — records material, labour and overhead costs for that job.
Overhead absorbed using OAR × actual hours for that job.
Selling Price
Total Job Cost + Mark-up % OR Total Cost ÷ (1 − Margin %)
02
Batch Costing
▶
Batch Costing
Similar to job costing but for a group (batch) of identical units. Total batch cost calculated, then divided by units.
Cost per Unit in Batch
Total Batch Cost ÷ Number of Units in Batch
03
Spoilage, Defectives & Scrap
▶
Spoilage
Units that CANNOT be economically reworked. Permanently unusable — scrapped. Cost absorbed into good units (normal) or charged to P&L (abnormal).
Defective Units
Units that CAN be reworked to meet quality standards. Rework cost = factory overhead (normal defectives) or P&L loss (abnormal).
Scrap
Material residue with minor value (e.g. metal filings, wood offcuts). Not a whole unit. Scrap revenue reduces factory overhead cost.
Key distinction exam loves to test: Spoilage = cannot rework. Defective = can rework. Scrap = not a unit at all, just material leftovers.
04
Job Pricing — Mark-up vs Margin KEY
▶
Mark-up (profit on COST)
SP = Cost × (1 + Mark-up %)  →  e.g. Cost Rs.100 + 25% = SP Rs.125
Margin (profit on SALES)
Profit = SP × Margin %  →  e.g. SP Rs.125 × 20% = Profit Rs.25
Convert Mark-up → Margin (exam formula)
Margin % = Mark-up % ÷ (100 + Mark-up %) × 100
25% mark-up → 25 ÷ 125 × 100 = 20% margin
25% mark-up on cost: If cost=100 → SP=125 → Profit on sale = 25/125 = 20%. Mark-up is always bigger % than its equivalent margin.
Shortcut: told "cost + X%", profit on sale = X ÷ (100+X) × 100. Always divide profit by SELLING PRICE for margin.
Used in manufacturing where products are made in batches (e.g. bread, electronics).
📝 Chapter Summary

Job costing accumulates costs for unique, customer-specific orders; batch costing applies the same logic to a batch of identical units, then averages the cost per unit.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Job Cost = Direct Material + Direct Labour + Applied Overhead (OAR × actual base for that job).
  • Batch cost per unit = Total batch cost ÷ number of units in the batch.
  • Spoilage = units that CANNOT be reworked. Defectives = units that CAN be reworked. Scrap = leftover material residue.
  • Mark-up % (on cost) is NOT the same as Margin % (on sales) — Margin = Mark-up ÷ (100 + Mark-up) × 100.
Chapter 10
Service Costing
01
Features of Service Costing
▶
Intangibility — Services cannot be touched or stored (e.g. haircut, consulting).
Simultaneity — Service is produced and consumed at the same time.
Heterogeneity — Each service delivery is slightly different (hard to standardise).
Perishability — Cannot be stored; unused capacity is lost forever (e.g. empty hotel room).
02
Cost per Service Unit
▶
Cost per Service Unit
Total Cost ÷ Number of Service Units
Service unit examples: cost per passenger-km (transport), cost per bed-night (hotel), cost per patient-day (hospital).
Composite cost unit — Two measures combined (e.g. passenger-km = passengers × km travelled).
📝 Chapter Summary

Service organisations use composite cost units because their output is intangible, heterogeneous, simultaneous, and perishable (SHIP) — making conventional unit costing impossible.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • SHIP = Simultaneity, Heterogeneity, Intangibility, Perishability — the 4 defining features of services.
  • Indirect/overhead costs form a HIGH proportion of total service cost.
  • Composite cost unit = two measures combined, e.g. passenger-km, bed-night, patient-day, tonne-km.
  • Cost per (composite) unit = Total cost ÷ number of composite units.
Chapter 11
Process Costing
01
What is Process Costing?
▶
Process Costing
Used when identical units are produced in a continuous process. Costs collected per process then averaged over all units. (e.g. chemicals, oil refining, food processing)
02
Normal Loss, Abnormal Loss & Abnormal Gain
▶
Normal Loss
Expected loss in a process. Its cost is absorbed by good output. Not shown as expense — built into cost per unit.
Abnormal Loss
Loss more than expected. Shown as a loss in P&L. Valued at same cost per unit as good output.
Abnormal Gain
Loss less than expected. Shown as a gain in P&L. Valued at same cost per unit as good output.
Cost per unit of expected output
(Total Process Cost − Scrap Value of Normal Loss) ÷ Expected Output
03
Equivalent Units (WIP)
▶
Equivalent Units — WIP converted to equivalent completed units. e.g. 100 units 60% complete = 60 equivalent units.
Used when there is opening or closing Work-in-Progress (WIP) in the process.
Two methods: FIFO (opening WIP finished first) and Weighted Average (blend old and new costs).
📝 Chapter Summary

Process costing averages costs over homogeneous units produced continuously; the equivalent units concept converts partly-finished WIP into 'whole unit' equivalents so costs can be fairly shared.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Equivalent Units = Fully completed units + (Closing WIP units × % completion).
  • Cost per Equivalent Unit = Total cost ÷ Equivalent units — calculated SEPARATELY for material and conversion cost.
  • Joint products are allocated cost using NRV method, Physical Units method, or Sales Value at Split-off.
  • By-product net revenue is usually DEDUCTED from the main product's joint cost.
Chapter 12
Standard Costing
01
What is a Standard Cost?
▶
Standard Cost
A pre-determined expected cost for producing one unit, based on efficient operation. Used for budgeting and control.
Set by engineers, accountants, and production managers together.
A Standard Cost Card shows the expected cost of material, labour, and overheads per unit.
02
Types of Standards
▶
Ideal Standard — Perfect conditions, no waste, no downtime. Impossible to achieve. Demotivating.
Attainable Standard — Based on efficient but realistic conditions. Some allowance for waste. Most motivating.
Current Standard — Based on current conditions including current inefficiencies. Not motivating.
Basic Standard — Set years ago, kept unchanged as a long-term base. Rarely used.
03
Uses & Limitations of Standard Costing
▶
Uses: budgeting, pricing, performance measurement, stock valuation, motivating employees.
Limitations: costly to set up, less useful in rapidly changing environments, may cause blame culture.
Less relevant in modern manufacturing with automation and JIT environments.
📝 Chapter Summary

Standard costing sets predetermined 'should-cost' figures for material, labour and overhead per unit, then compares them with actual costs to compute variances for control and management by exception.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Ideal standard = perfect conditions (rarely achievable). Attainable standard = allows for normal waste/idle time — most commonly used.
  • Standard costing can be combined with EITHER job costing OR process costing.
  • General variance formula: Variance = Standard Cost (for actual output) − Actual Cost.
  • Variances enable 'management by exception' — only significant deviations need investigation.
Chapter 13
Variance Analysis: Materials & Labour
01
What is a Variance?
▶
Variance
The difference between standard and actual cost. Favourable (F) = actual better than standard. Adverse (A) = actual worse than standard.
02
Material Variances KEY
▶
Total Material Cost Variance
(Standard Cost of Actual Output) − (Actual Cost)
Material Price Variance
(Standard Price − Actual Price) × Actual Quantity Purchased
Material Usage Variance
(Standard Quantity for Actual Output − Actual Quantity Used) × Standard Price
Price Variance + Usage Variance = Total Material Cost Variance.
03
Labour Variances KEY
▶
Total Labour Cost Variance
(Standard Labour Cost of Actual Output) − (Actual Labour Cost)
Labour Rate Variance
(Standard Rate − Actual Rate) × Actual Hours Paid
Labour Efficiency Variance
(Standard Hours for Actual Output − Actual Hours Worked) × Standard Rate
Idle Time Variance
Idle Hours × Standard Rate (always Adverse)
04
Variance Causes
▶
Material Price (Adv) — Supplier raised prices, rushed purchase, different quality bought.
Material Usage (Adv) — Wastage, poor quality material, theft, untrained workers.
Labour Rate (Adv) — Higher paid workers used, overtime, wage rate increase.
Labour Efficiency (Adv) — Untrained workers, machine breakdowns, poor supervision.
📝 Chapter Summary

Material and labour variances split the overall cost variance into a price/rate component and a usage/efficiency component, helping pinpoint exactly where the deviation occurred.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Material Price Variance = (Std Price − Actual Price) × Actual Quantity purchased/used.
  • Material Usage Variance = (Std Qty for actual output − Actual Qty used) × Std Price.
  • Labour Rate Variance = (Std Rate − Actual Rate) × Actual Hours worked.
  • Labour Efficiency Variance = (Std Hours for actual output − Actual Hours worked) × Std Rate.
Chapter 14
Variance Analysis: Overheads
01
Variable Overhead Variances
▶
Variable OH Expenditure Variance
(Actual Hours × Std Variable OH Rate) − Actual Variable OH Cost
Variable OH Efficiency Variance
(Std Hours for Actual Output − Actual Hours) × Std Variable OH Rate
Expenditure = was each hour more/less expensive than standard?
Efficiency = were more/fewer hours used than standard?
02
Fixed Overhead Variances KEY
▶
Fixed OH Total Variance
Absorbed Fixed OH − Actual Fixed OH
Fixed OH Expenditure Variance
Budgeted Fixed OH − Actual Fixed OH
Fixed OH Volume Variance
Absorbed Fixed OH − Budgeted Fixed OH
Volume Efficiency Variance
(Std Hours for Actual Output − Actual Hours) × Fixed OH Rate
Volume Capacity Variance
(Actual Hours − Budgeted Hours) × Fixed OH Rate
03
Variance Investigation
▶
Not every variance is investigated — only if it is significant (material in size or %).
Management uses exception reporting — focus only on variances outside acceptable range.
Variances can be caused by: planning errors (wrong standards) or operational issues (real inefficiency).
📝 Chapter Summary

Overhead variances explain the gap between overhead absorbed into production and actual overhead incurred, separating spending (expenditure) issues from activity-level (volume/efficiency) issues.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Fixed OH Expenditure Variance = Budgeted Fixed OH − Actual Fixed OH.
  • Fixed OH Volume Variance = (Actual output − Budgeted output) × Std Fixed OH rate per unit.
  • Variable OH Expenditure Variance compares actual variable OH with the budget allowance for actual hours worked.
  • Total OH Variance = Absorbed OH − Actual OH = sum of all the individual OH variances.
Chapter 15
Sales Variance & Profit Reconciliation
01
Sales Variances
▶
Sales Price Variance
(Actual Price − Standard Price) × Actual Volume Sold
Sales Volume Variance (Profit)
(Actual Volume − Budgeted Volume) × Standard Profit per unit
Sales Volume Variance (Contribution)
(Actual Volume − Budgeted Volume) × Standard Contribution per unit
Sold at higher price than standard = Favourable price variance.
Sold more units than budget = Favourable volume variance.
02
Operating Statement (Profit Reconciliation) KEY
▶
Operating Statement reconciles Budgeted Profit to Actual Profit by listing all variances.
Operating Statement Structure
Budgeted Profit + Favourable Variances − Adverse Variances = Actual Profit
Variances included: Sales Price, Sales Volume, Material (Price + Usage), Labour (Rate + Efficiency), OH variances.
Final figure should equal actual profit calculated from actual results.
03
Mix & Yield Variances (Advanced)
▶
Mix Variance — When different proportions of materials are used vs standard mix. Caused by substituting one material for another.
Yield Variance — When actual output from the actual mix differs from expected output. (More or less output than expected)
Mix + Yield Variances together = Material Usage Variance.
📝 Chapter Summary

Sales variances measure how price and volume changes affected revenue/profit versus budget; profit reconciliation then ties the budgeted profit to the actual profit through every variance calculated.

⭐ Most Important Points
  • Sales Price Variance = Actual Sales Revenue − (Actual Quantity sold × Standard/Budgeted Price).
  • Sales Volume Variance = (Actual Qty sold − Budgeted Qty) × Standard Profit (or Contribution) per unit.
  • For multiple products, Sales Volume Variance splits further into Sales Mix Variance + Sales Quantity Variance.
  • Profit Reconciliation: Budgeted Profit + Total Favourable Variances − Total Adverse Variances = Actual Profit.

D3
Name and explain 5 types of Sampling Methods
▶
PAST PAPER

1. Random Sampling — Every item has equal chance of selection. Most unbiased. E.g. lottery method.

2. Systematic Sampling — Select every nth item from a list. E.g. every 10th invoice. Simple and efficient.

3. Stratified Sampling — Divide population into groups (strata) by characteristic, then randomly sample each stratum in proportion. Most representative.

4. Cluster Sampling — Divide population into geographic clusters, randomly select entire clusters. Cheaper but less accurate.

5. Multi-stage Sampling — Sampling done in multiple stages with random selection at each stage. Used for large nationwide surveys.

▶
PAST PAPER

Qualitative Data — Descriptive; non-numerical; about characteristics, opinions, qualities.
Examples: customer satisfaction rating descriptions, product quality assessments, employee morale surveys.

Management accounting uses both: financial figures (quantitative) AND non-financial measures like customer satisfaction scores (qualitative).

What is Prime Cost, Conversion Cost, Factory Cost? (MOST REPEATED)

VERY HIGH · EVERY PAPER

D2
Variable Cost, Fixed Cost and Semi-Variable Cost — define with behaviour
▶
VERY HIGH

Variable Cost — Increases in TOTAL with output. SAME per unit. Example: raw material.

Fixed Cost — SAME in total regardless of output. DECREASES per unit as output rises. Example: rent.

Semi-Variable (Mixed) — Has both fixed + variable parts. Example: phone bill = fixed line rental + variable call charges.

Stepped Fixed Cost — Fixed until a level, then jumps up. Example: need extra supervisor after 50 workers.

Key exam trap: Fixed cost total = fixed. Fixed cost per unit = variable (changes). Variable cost total = variable. Variable cost per unit = fixed (stays same).
D3
Direct Cost vs Indirect Cost — define with examples
▶
PAST PAPER

Direct Cost — Can be traced to a specific product/job.
• Direct Material: wood used in furniture
• Direct Labour: factory worker wages
• Direct Expenses: hiring machine for one specific job

Indirect Cost (Overhead) — Cannot be traced to one product. Shared among many.
• Indirect Material: lubricating oil, cleaning supplies
• Indirect Labour: supervisor salary, security guard
• Indirect Expenses: factory rent, electricity, insurance
D4
Product Cost vs Period Cost — define and differentiate
▶
PAST PAPER

Product Cost — Attached to the product. Stays as inventory (asset) until sold. Includes: direct material, direct labour, production overhead.

Period Cost — Charged to P&L in the period it occurs, regardless of sales. Includes: admin cost, selling cost, distribution cost.

Rule: Production = Product Cost. Everything else = Period Cost.

▶

Uncontrollable Cost — Cannot be controlled by a specific manager. Example: rent, taxation, insurance, inflation.

Opportunity Cost — Value of the next best alternative given up when making a decision. Not a cash cost — never in accounting records.

Sunk Cost — Already spent, cannot be recovered. Irrelevant for future decision making. Example: market research already done.

Define FIFO and Weighted Average Cost (AVCO) — with advantages and disadvantages
▶
AUG 2024 PAPERVERY HIGH

FIFO (First In, First Out) — Oldest stock issued first. Closing stock = latest prices.
✅ Realistic inventory values (near current prices)
✅ Easy to understand
❌ In rising prices → higher profit → higher tax
❌ Old prices used for cost of sales (not current)

AVCO (Weighted Average Cost) — New average calculated after each receipt. Issues at average price.
Formula: New Average = (Old Stock Value + New Receipt Value) ÷ Total Units
✅ Smooths out price fluctuations
✅ Accepted under IAS 2
✅ Best for goods that cannot be separated (oil, wheat)
❌ Average may not reflect current market price

D3
EOQ — define and give formula
▶
JUNE 2024–FEB 2025VERY HIGH

EOQ (Economic Order Quantity) — The order quantity that minimises TOTAL inventory costs (ordering + holding costs combined). At EOQ: ordering cost = holding cost.

EOQ Formula
√ (2 × Annual Demand × Ordering Cost per order ÷ Holding Cost per unit per year)
Higher holding cost → smaller EOQ (order less, more often).
Higher demand or ordering cost → larger EOQ.
D4
Stock Level Formulas — ROL, Min, Max, Average
▶
PAST PAPER · NUMERICAL

Reorder Level (ROL)
Maximum Usage × Maximum Lead Time
Minimum Level (Buffer Stock)
Reorder Level − (Average Usage × Average Lead Time)
Maximum Level
Reorder Level + EOQ − (Minimum Usage × Minimum Lead Time)
Average Stock
Minimum Level + ½ EOQ
Stock Turnover
Issues during period ÷ Average Stock [(Max + Min) ÷ 2]
Lead Time = gap between placing order and receiving delivery.

Define: Overhead Allocation, Apportionment and Absorption (OAR) — EVERY PAPER
▶
OCT 2024DEC 2024EVERY PAPER

Allocation — When an overhead can be charged ENTIRELY to one specific cost centre. (e.g. depreciation of machine only in Dept A → allocated to Dept A)

Apportionment — When an overhead is SHARED among departments on a fair basis. (e.g. rent split by floor area)

Reapportionment — Service dept costs transferred to production depts (they don't make products).

Overhead Absorption (OAR) — Adding a share of overhead to each unit of product using OAR.
OAR Formula
Budgeted Overhead ÷ Budgeted Activity Level (hours/units)
D2

▶

Why inappropriate:
• Different departments have different overhead costs and activity levels
• Some depts are labour-intensive, others machine-intensive
• One rate cannot fairly represent all departments
• Products using more of one expensive dept will be under-costed
• Leads to inaccurate product costs and wrong pricing decisions

Over-absorption and Under-absorption — causes and treatment
▶

Causes: Actual activity different from budgeted, actual costs different from budgeted, both together.

📋 Descriptive Questions
D1
Difference between Absorption Costing and Marginal (Variable) Costing — EVERY PAPER
▶
AUG 2024AUG 2023EVERY PAPER

Absorption Costing:
• Both fixed AND variable production costs in product cost
• Fixed overhead absorbed into each unit (product cost)
• Required by IAS 2 for external reporting
• Higher closing stock value (fixed OH included)
• Higher profit when production > sales

Marginal (Variable) Costing:
• Only variable production costs in product cost
• Fixed costs = period cost (all charged to P&L immediately)
• Better for internal decision-making
• Lower closing stock value
• Lower profit when production > sales

When production = sales: BOTH give same profit.
Profit Reconciliation (Marginal → Absorption)
Marginal Profit ± (Change in Stock × Fixed OH per unit) = Absorption Profit
D2
If produced units EXCEED sales units — which method shows HIGHER operating income?
▶
AUG 2024 PAPER

Absorption Costing shows higher operating income.

Why? When production > sales, more units are in closing stock under absorption costing. Fixed overhead is "carried forward" in stock, so less fixed cost hits the P&L. This makes absorption profit higher than marginal profit.
D3
How is fixed manufacturing cost transferred to next period under absorption costing?
▶
AUG 2023

Under absorption costing, fixed overhead is included in the cost of each unit produced.

If units are NOT sold → fixed overhead stays in closing inventory (balance sheet as asset).

When those units are sold in the NEXT period → fixed overhead moves from inventory to Cost of Goods Sold in P&L.

This is how fixed costs "travel" with unsold inventory to the next period.
D4
What are the causes of difference in operating profit between absorption and marginal costing? (Feb 2025)
▶
FEB 2025 PAPER

The ONLY cause of difference = change in inventory level.

Under absorption costing, fixed overhead is included in stock value. When stock levels change, different amounts of fixed OH hit the P&L.

• Production > Sales → Stock increases → Absorption profit > Marginal profit
• Production < Sales → Stock decreases → Absorption profit < Marginal profit
• Production = Sales → No stock change → Both profits equal

D2
Define Normal Loss, Abnormal Loss and Abnormal Gain — with accounting treatment
▶
OCT 2024DEC 2024VERY HIGH

Normal Loss — EXPECTED loss in a process (based on experience). Its cost is absorbed by good output — NOT shown as expense separately. If scrap value exists, credited to process account.

Abnormal Loss — Loss MORE than expected (actual loss > normal loss). Treated as P&L expense (adverse). Valued at SAME cost per unit as good output.

Abnormal Gain — Loss LESS than expected (actual loss < normal loss). Treated as P&L income (favourable). Valued at SAME cost per unit as good output.

Cost per unit of expected output
(Total Process Cost − Scrap Value of Normal Loss) ÷ Expected Output

▶
DEC 2024

Equivalent Production — WIP converted to equivalent WHOLE units for cost calculation purposes.

Formula: Equivalent Units = Actual WIP Units × % Completion
Example: 100 units 60% complete = 60 equivalent units

Used to fairly calculate cost per unit when there is opening or closing WIP in the process.

▶

By-Products — Minor product created accidentally during main process. LOW value. Revenue from by-product reduces main product cost. Example: sawdust from timber cutting, molasses from sugar refining.

Standard Cost — definition, with example
▶
JUNE 2024–FEB 2025 FEEDBACKHIGH

Standard Cost — A pre-determined EXPECTED cost per unit, set before production begins, based on efficient working conditions.

D2
4 Types of Standards — define each
▶
PAST PAPER

Ideal Standard — Perfect conditions, zero waste, no downtime. Impossible to achieve. Demotivating for staff.

Attainable Standard — Realistic with efficient working. Some allowance for normal waste/idle time. Most motivating. ✅ Best type.

Current Standard — Based on current conditions including current inefficiencies. Not motivating (rewards inefficiency).

Basic Standard — Set years ago, kept unchanged as long-term base for trend comparison. Rarely used for control.

📋 Descriptive Questions
D1
Sales Price Variance (SPV) and Sales Volume Variance (SVV) — define and give formulas
▶
JUNE 2024–FEB 2025 FEEDBACKVERY HIGH

Sales Price Variance (SPV)
(Actual Price − Standard Price) × Actual Units Sold
Higher actual price than standard = Favourable. Lower = Adverse.

Sales Volume Variance (SVV)
(Actual Volume − Budgeted Volume) × Standard Profit per unit
Sold more than budget = Favourable. Sold less = Adverse.
D2
How to calculate Material Quantity (Usage) Variance
▶
AUG 2024

Material Usage (Quantity) Variance
(Standard Quantity for Actual Output − Actual Quantity Used) × Standard Price
Used less material than standard = Favourable.
Used more material than standard = Adverse.

Causes (Adverse): Wastage, poor quality material, untrained workers, theft.
Causes (Favourable): Better quality material, skilled workers, less waste.
D3
Idle Time Variance — definition and formula
▶
PAST PAPERHIGH

Idle Time Variance — Cost of time workers were paid but NOT producing.

Idle Time Variance
Idle Hours × Standard Rate per Hour (ALWAYS ADVERSE)
Causes: machine breakdown, waiting for materials, power failure.
Always adverse — you can never have a favourable idle time variance.
D4
Operating Statement — what is it and its structure
▶
OCT 2024DEC 2024HIGH

Operating Statement — Reconciles Budgeted Profit to Actual Profit by listing all variances.

Operating Statement Structure
Budgeted Profit
+ Favourable Variances (Sales Price, Sales Volume)
− Adverse Variances (Material, Labour, OH)
= Actual Profit
All variances included: Sales Price, Sales Volume, Material Price, Material Usage, Labour Rate, Labour Efficiency, FOH Expenditure, FOH Volume.
D5
Relation between Labour Rate Variance and Labour Efficiency Variance (Dec 2024)
▶
DEC 2024 PAPER

Labour Rate Variance = difference between actual rate paid and standard rate × actual hours paid. Relates to the PRICE paid per hour.

Labour Efficiency Variance = difference between standard hours and actual hours × standard rate. Relates to HOW QUICKLY work was done.

Relationship: They can be interrelated — e.g. hiring cheaper (lower-skilled) workers may give a favourable rate variance, but those workers may be slower, causing an adverse efficiency variance.
